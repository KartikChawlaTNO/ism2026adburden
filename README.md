## Appendix A: Compliance Regimes for an EU Intermediate Stainless-Steel Producer

This appendix maps the principal compliance regimes that apply to an EU producer of intermediate stainless steel, drawing on the JRC Steel DPP Content Proposal, the relevant regulatory texts, and industry standards. It corresponds to the G1 stage (identifying compliance requirements) of the compliance lifecycle.

### G1: Identifying compliance requirements

| Reporting Scheme | Reporting type | Key data required | Granularity | Frequency | Applicability to the OneBlade stainless-steel case study |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR)** | Mandatory and Voluntary | **Mandatory:**<br>• Unique Product Identifier (HEAT Number, Model-level Identifier, CAST/LOT No. if applicable) & Serial No.<br>• Other Identifiers (GTIN, TARIC Code, HS/CN Code, Purchase order)<br>• Product Classification (Product ESPR Category, Steel Grade Classification, Steel designation, Technology Route)<br>• Producer Identification (Unique Facility Identifier, Unique Operator Identifier; manufacturer / authorised representative / importer name, address, trademark, contact; other Operator Identifier)<br>• Origin (Country of origin, melt & pour, manufacturing date)<br>• Name or numerical code of Substance of Concern (IUPAC code, other names, EC number, CAS number)<br>• Location of substance (not applicable or surface treatment)<br>• Concentration (Concentration description, value and units)<br>• Instructions for safe use (Standard 'safe for use' content proposed)<br>• Product Carbon Footprint (Carbon footprint declaration & conformity declaration)<br>• Recycled content (Recycled content declaration, pre-/post-consumer material split, conformity declaration)<br><br>**Voluntary:**<br>• Full scope of the MTC following EN 10204:2004<br>• CBAM Report ID<br>• CPR Declaration of Performance<br>• REACH Documentation | Generally Model or Batch; Item-level UPI in specific cases; Operator-level granularity for technology route | When product is placed on the market | **Applicable.** The intermediate stainless-steel components are likely to fall under the anticipated delegated act for intermediate steel. Reporting at lot and heat level may not be required for the final consumer product; applicability at that level depends on future regulations and product category. |
| **Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM)**<br>EU 2023/956<br>Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2023/1773 | Mandatory (CBAM Art. 35) | **CBAM Art. 6(2)(a):** Total quantity of goods imported<br>**CBAM Art. 6(2)(b):** Embedded emissions<br>**CBAM Art. 6(2)(c):** Total number of CBAM certificates to be surrendered<br>**CBAM Art. 6(2)(d) & Art. 8:** Copies of verification reports<br>**CBAM Art. 9 (optional):** Carbon price paid in country of origin<br><br>**Additional data under Implementing Regulation Art. 3(2):**<br>• Country of origin<br>• Production installation<br>• Production route<br>• Precursor emissions<br>• Electricity emissions factor and calculation method<br>• Identification number of the specific steel mill, if known | Installation, per CN Code | Quarterly in transition period (CBAM Art. 35); annual from 1 January 2026, first declaration due 31 May 2027 (CBAM Art. 6) | **Only applicable if materials are imported.** CBAM applies to imports of steel products from non-EU sources. In this case, the OneBlade uses EU-sourced steel and would likely not fall under CBAM. However, the regime is relevant for similar products sourcing steel from outside the EU (e.g. Chinese imports). |
| **EU Emissions Trading Scheme**<br>Directive 2003/87/EC<br>Implementing Regulation (EU) 2018/2066 (MRR)<br>Implementing Regulation (EU) 2018/2067 (AVR) | Mandatory | **MRR Art. 68 + Annex X:**<br>• Identification of installation and verifier<br>• Reference to monitoring plan<br>• Any relevant changes<br>• Annual verified greenhouse gas emissions<br>• Source streams<br>• Activity data<br>• Calculation factors<br>• Tier levels<br>• Biomass portions, if applicable<br>• Any data caps | Installation | Annual | **Indirectly relevant, and only at the installation level.** EU ETS applies to the steel production installation, not to the product. If the steel supplier's installation reports under EU ETS, this implies more data is available — and more reliable — which improves the reliability of downstream LCA data. There is therefore an indirect relationship, but no direct product-level obligation. |
| **REACH** (Regulation 1907/2006) | Mandatory | • Identity, concentration, and location within the article of substances of very high concern<br>• Safe-use information | Per article placed on the market | When placed on the market | **Applicable.** Substances of very high concern in stainless-steel alloys must be identified and communicated downstream. |
| **Construction Products Regulation** (Regulation 2024/3110) | Mandatory | • Declaration of Performance and Conformity<br>• Technical documentation against harmonised standards including EN 10025 and EN 10088<br>• Environmental sustainability performance | Product Model level | When placed on the market | **Not applicable.** The OneBlade is a consumer product, not a construction product. The CPR does not apply to this case. |
| **Mill Testing Certificates** (EN 10204:2004) | Voluntary | • Chemical composition<br>• Mechanical properties<br>• Dimensions<br>• Steel grade | Batch level (Heat / cast / bundle) or item level (coil or sheet) | Per delivery | **Applicable.** Standard practice for steel deliveries; provides batch-level composition and mechanical property data used in downstream compliance. |
| **Environmental Product Declarations** (EN 15804) | Voluntary | • General information<br>• Environmental impact indicators<br>• Resource and material indicators<br>• Waste and output flows | Product family | Every five years (EN 15804) | **Applicable.** Where the steel producer maintains an EPD, the product-level carbon footprint data can be reused in the DPP and downstream LCA. |
| **International Material Data System (IMDS)** | Voluntary | • Material Composition | Batch level | — | **Not applicable.** IMDS is primarily used in the automotive industry for material composition reporting. It does not apply to consumer electronics such as the OneBlade. |

## Appendix B: Candidate Solutions for Compliance Automation Across the Stainless-Steel Pipeline

This appendix details the candidate solutions identified in Section 4.2, grouped by the lifecycle stage at which they act (G1 Interpretation, G2 Integration, G3 Documentation). For each solution, the table gives the actor that operates it (operator O, authority A, or both), a definition and source, its application to the carbon-disclosure example in the OneBlade case, its output class (regulatory guidance, compliance data, or infrastructure) and Level of DPP Verifiability where applicable, and its principal benefits (+), costs (€), and risks (−). AI-related risks are coded R (capability and robustness) and T (transparency and interpretability) following Slattery et al. (2024).

| # | Stage-Actor | Candidate solution, definition & examples | In the OneBlade case (carbon disclosure) | Output class | Benefits (+), Costs (€), Risks (−) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| #1 | G1-O | **Legal-support LLMs (RAG / neuro-symbolic).** Language models that extract obligations from legal text and draft schema fields, using methods such as retrieval-augmented generation or neuro-symbolic design to ground outputs. | Extracting the carbon-disclosure obligations (PCF; embedded emissions) from the ESPR delegated act, CBAM and EU ETS texts into candidate DPP schema fields | Regulatory guidance {Non-authoritative} | + Rapid conversion of legal text into structured, reusable schemas; enables audit trail from field to source article<br>− R: misread clauses, wrong/missing fields<br>− R: AI may overstate compliance requirements<br>− T: rationale not reconstructable<br>€ Human review, versioning and maintenance; AI risk-management requirements |
| #2 | G1/2-O | **Machine-executable DPP Obligation Vocabularies.** Shared, machine-executable semantic expressions of DPP requirements in controlled vocabularies. Published and maintained by (a) independent organisations, (b) collectives (e.g. CIRPASS vocabularies), (c) standard-setting organisations (CEN-CENELEC SMART; IEC SMART Utility Model; BAM.de QI Smart Standards), or (d) regulators (DPP Registry Regulation; UNTP Sustainability Vocabulary Catalog, UNECE). | Expressing the steel DPP's carbon fields (PCF; embedded emissions per tonne) in a shared machine-executable vocabulary: (a) independently, (b) via CIRPASS, (c) via CEN-CENELEC, or (d) via the DPP Registry | Regulatory guidance {Non-authoritative if published independently; Authoritative if published by a standard-setting organisation or regulator}<br><br>Infrastructure | + Shared, unambiguous expression of legal requirements, reusable across organisations, potentially integrating multiple legislations<br>+ One (potentially) authoritative interpretation, reusable by complying entities and authorities ("specify once, reuse many")<br>+ A crucial enabler for many of the other solutions listed in this table<br>€ Depends on a maintained, authoritative vocabulary, borne by the publishing entity<br>€ Interoperability between vocabularies must also be maintained<br>€ Machine-executability requires compatible systems |
| #3 | G2-O | **Consolidated dataset ("report-once").** A single dataset of compliance data, reused across compliance regimes, applying the EU once-only principle. | A per-plant carbon dataset (fuel and process inputs, electricity, output tonnage) feeding CBAM, EU ETS and EPD carbon disclosures | Compliance data (LoV is inherited) | + Container — carries upstream data<br>€ Requires legislative alignment or automated translation<br>− Errors may propagate to every derived report |
| #4 | G2-O | **Intermediate-to-final DPP integration.** Transfer of compliance data from an upstream (intermediate) DPP into a downstream product DPP. | Carrying the steel's cradle-to-gate carbon footprint from the intermediate steel DPP into the shaver's final-product carbon footprint | Compliance data (LoV is inherited) | + Upstream data need not be re-produced downstream<br>€ Requires a DPP ecosystem across the chain<br>− Data only as valid as its first entry; liability across the transfer must be assigned |
| #5 | G2-O / G3-A | **Vision-language models (VLMs).** Vision-native AI interpreting text and layout to convert legacy PDF certificates into machine-readable data. Used by complying entities to ingest compliance data into compliance documentation, and by authorities to review supplied technical documentation. | Converting an upstream supplier's energy/emissions certificate or EPD PDF into machine-readable data points; on the authority side, reviewing provided certification | Compliance data<br>LoV 0 for AI-extracted data; LoV 2–4 for accompanying original data | + Layout-aware conversion without manual re-keying<br>+ Enables data ingestion or assessment at scale<br>− R: hallucinated/misread values; rises with autonomy<br>− T: derivation hard to reconstruct<br>€ Compute-intensive; human validation needed |
| #6 | G2/3-O | **Continuous Controls Monitoring (CCM).** Automated, continuous monitoring and testing of controls, recording compliance data and deviations as they occur, paired with options for remediation and decision-gates (Vohradsky, 2015). Maintains tamper-evident, time-stamped records. With Digital Quality Infrastructure, calibration and reference-material certificates are digitalised so calibration paths and measurement uncertainty travel with the data. Parts of the process may be AI-assisted. Subsumes one-button trusted release, digital quality infrastructure, and digital audit log. | Continuously monitoring metered energy and emissions (e.g. CEMS), capturing deviations, and gating release of a coil on a complete, conforming carbon disclosure before release | Compliance data<br>LoV 1–5 | + Values qualify as measured rather than declared; metrological context travels with the data<br>+ Evidence accumulates as a managed process; enables release-gating<br>+ Tamper-evident, time-stamped records can be created<br>+ Reports are automatically generated<br>− Coverage gaps where un-instrumented<br>− Instrument errors may occur<br>− Certification of the associated digital infrastructure by market authorities may be required<br>€ Monitoring rules must be complete and maintained<br>€ Compatible infrastructure is required<br>€ Requires upstream digitalisation and integration with production systems |
| #7 | G2-O/A | **Shape Validation.** Automated validation of DPP data against a shape / data model confirming required fields are present and well-formed (W3C SHACL; CIRPASS-2 RED tool). | Checking that the carbon fields (PCF; embedded emissions) are present and well-formed | Infrastructure | + Assessment — at-scale completeness check; weeds out trivial errors<br>− Confirms presence, not truth; gameable where rules are public and static |
| #8 | G3-O | **(European) Identity Wallets and Verifiable Credentials.** Holder-controlled business or individual digital identity wallets for issuing, holding and presenting signed credentials, establishing identities and revocable, attributable compliance data points bound to an identifier and role (European Digital Identity Wallet; European Business Wallet; eIDAS; W3C VC Data Model). | Signing and presenting the carbon-footprint declaration bound to the producer's identity and authorised role | Infrastructure {Attribution} | + Carrier — holder-controlled issuing and presentation of credentials<br>− Framework still under development and adoption<br>€ Significant infrastructure adoption and maintenance required |
| #9 | G3-A | **Authority-side analytics & dashboards.** Supervisory analytics over many DPPs (registry access, data-lake ingestion, crawling); an application of SupTech. | Cross-checking declared carbon footprints across many steel DPPs for outliers and trends | Infrastructure<br>LoV 6 {System-level attestation analytics} | + Continuous, system-level oversight<br>+ Trend analysis, also as input for regulatory processes<br>− T: data must remain verifiable and attributable after aggregation; an aggregate is no more reliable than its weakest input<br>− Does not replace spot-checks and audits |
| #10 | G3-A | **Plausibility assessments.** Automated screening of values against plausible ranges and required reporting thresholds. | Screening the declared embedded-emissions value against sector benchmarks for plausibility | Infrastructure<br>LoV 1 {Data point verification} | + Cheap first-pass screen for gross errors<br>− Plausible-but-false values pass; gameable where thresholds are known |

## Appendix C: Practitioner Survey Results

This appendix presents the results of the practitioner survey conducted on 17 June 2026 with 18 respondents. Respondents were asked to rate each candidate solution along two dimensions: its perceived potential to enable DPP-mediated compliance automation, and their organisation's comfort relying on it.

### Q1: How does your organization currently manage compliance with multiple EU regulations?

![Q1: Current compliance management approaches](https://github.com/KartikChawlaTNO/ism2026adburden/blob/main/Picture1.png)

### Q2: Perceived potential of candidate solutions for enabling automated compliance through DPP

![Q2: Potential of candidate solutions for enabling automated compliance](https://github.com/KartikChawlaTNO/ism2026adburden/blob/main/Picture3.png)

**Key insights:**
- Consolidated dataset ("report-once") and machine-executable DPP obligation vocabularies have the highest perceived potential and strongest consensus among respondents.
- Continuous Controls Monitoring and intermediate-to-final DPP integration have decent potential, but high variation among respondents.
- AI-mediated solutions (vision-language models, legal-support LLMs) have lower overall potential and high standard deviation, indicating skepticism.
- Digital Identity Wallets scored the lowest perceived potential.

### Q3: Organisational comfort relying on these solutions for compliance and validation

![Q3: Organisational comfort levels in relying on automated solutions](https://github.com/KartikChawlaTNO/ism2026adburden/blob/main/Picture4.png)

**Key insights:**
- The dominant sentiment is that respondents are comfortable with the solutions provided they are "labelled and auditable."
- Plausibility assessments and shape validation are seen as low-risk and highly reliable.
- Consolidated dataset ("report-once") and machine-executable vocabularies have a high level of comfort — standardising the data layer is preferred and trusted.
- AI-related solutions and Digital Identity Wallets have lower comfort levels and higher standard deviation, indicating more skepticism.

### Strategic alignment: potential vs. comfort

![Strategic alignment matrix: technical potential vs. governance comfort](https://github.com/KartikChawlaTNO/ism2026adburden/blob/main/Picture5.png)

This matrix plots each candidate solution by its average perceived potential (x-axis) and average organisational comfort (y-axis). Machine-executable DPP obligation vocabularies and consolidated datasets cluster in the "Strategic Priority" quadrant (high potential, high trust). AI-mediated solutions and Digital Identity Wallets fall in or near the "High Potential Risk" and "Low Priority" quadrants, reflecting the gap between their technical capabilities and current organisational trust.
